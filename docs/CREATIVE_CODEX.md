## FOREWORD

This codex governs every visual and editorial decision made on behalf of Phaigort across all media: digital, print, video, event, and AI-generated photography. It is not a mood board. It is a constitution.

Every image produced under this document must answer one question before it is approved:

**Does this reveal what the material is, not merely what it looks like?**

If the answer is yes, it belongs in the Phaigort universe. If it merely decorates, it does not.

## PART ONE: THE CREATIVE CHARTER

### 1.1 — Brand Essence

Phaigort is a **Wonderhouse of Material Consciousness** — a term that must be understood precisely before any creative decision is made.

- **Wonderhouse**: Not a gallery, not a store, not a boutique. A place where wonder is the operating principle. Wonder is the feeling of encountering something whose scale or history or complexity exceeds your immediate frame of reference. Phaigort creates that encounter.
- **Material**: Physical, tangible, geological. The word material carries weight — it implies substance, evidence, truth. Phaigort traffics in objects that exist independently of human opinion about them.
- **Consciousness**: The frame through which material is understood. Phaigort does not simply present objects; it contextualises them within geological time, trade history, cultural heritage, and scientific understanding.

The brand draws its genetic material from the **Iberian Voyage** — the 15th–18th century Portuguese and Spanish maritime network through which materials from four continents first converged in a single market. The caravel ships, the trading posts of Goa and Malacca, the maps of Garcia de Orta, the Manila Galleon trade route — these are Phaigort's ancestral precedents.

Most critically: Garcia de Orta, the 16th-century Portuguese physician and naturalist, wrote the first scientific gemstone compendium *in everyday language — so that traders and other locals could make use of it.* This is the founding gesture of Phaigort. Knowledge about extraordinary materials should not be hoarded by experts. It should be shared with everyone curious enough to ask.

**Positioning statement**: *Accessible Premium with luxury appeals. Warm, inclusive, intellectually curious — not exclusive or intimidating. "For the genuinely curious," not "for the chosen few."*

### 1.4 — The Four Creative Tensions

These tensions are **productive**, not problems to be solved. They give Phaigort its character.

1. **Geological time ↔ Human moment**: A Kashmir sapphire is 65 million years old and can be held in a palm. Photography must honour both scales.
2. **Scientific precision ↔ Aesthetic wonder**: Technical accuracy must coexist with beauty. Neither sacrifices the other.
3. **Luxury aspiration ↔ Democratic access**: Objects of exceptional rarity presented with warmth and generosity. The language of curiosity, never the language of gatekeeping.
4. **Ancient materials ↔ Contemporary display**: Historical provenance in a living, modern digital context. Not a museum. A living institution.

## PART TWO: THE VISUAL IDENTITY SYSTEM

### 2.1 — Color Theology

Phaigort's palette is not decorative. Each colour carries a specific semantic weight.

#### Primary Field: Platinum White `#F8F9FB`
*Coverage: 60–75% of any composition.*

This is the breath between moments. The white of bleached Portuguese limestone. The light of an overcast Atlantic sky. It creates the void space that allows heavy materials to breathe. It is never pure white — it carries warmth (the `#FB` in the hex is slightly warm), preventing the clinical coldness of pure #FFFFFF.

**Photographic equivalent**: Aged vellum, bleached linen, white marble with grey veining, overcast studio light.

#### Anchor: Royal Navy `#1A2851`
*Coverage: 40–50% as text and DarkFieldStage blocks.*

The depth of oceanic water between the Tagus River and the Indian Ocean. The colour of night sky over open water. In dark-field photography, this is the background that makes gemstones float. As text, it carries the weight of the scholarly tradition — serious but not severe.

**Photographic equivalent**: Midnight blue velvet, deep-sea water, night sky without stars, indigo-dyed linen.

#### Life Signal: Coral Reef `#FF6B4A`
*Coverage: 5–8% maximum — CTAs, discovery markers, punctuation.*

Used with restraint equivalent to a red stamp on a trading document. The colour of a flame, of volcanic rock at the surface, of the moment something is found. It marks action and urgency without alarm. When used in photography as a prop or accent, it should appear as: a wax seal, a single coral fragment, a ceramic shard, fire.

**Never use coral as a background colour. Never use it for decorative purposes. Only for signal.**

#### Navigation: Sapphire Blue `#0F52BA`
*Coverage: Accent — links, interactive states.*

The blue of Iberian maritime charts. Deep sea, not sky. Used for navigation (links, interactive elements) because it is directional — it points somewhere.

#### Void Stage: Deep Navy `#0F172A`
*The DarkFieldStage background. The laboratory dark. The velvet beneath a gemstone.*

This is the colour of gemological dark-field illumination — the standard scientific technique for revealing internal structure in coloured stones. When Phaigort places content on this field, it is making a statement: *we are examining this with the rigour of a gemologist*.

### 2.2 — Typography Architecture

#### Cormorant — The Voice of Geological Time
**Role**: Headings, display text, editorial statements, pullquotes.
**Weight usage**: Bold (700) for declarations; Semibold (600) for headers; Italic for scientific or philosophical asides.
**Sizes**: Display (clamp 48–96px), H1 (clamp 36–72px), H2 (clamp 28–48px), H3 (clamp 22–36px).

Cormorant is an Old-Style serif with pronounced calligraphic contrast — its thick strokes and thin hairlines recall the manuscript hand of Renaissance natural philosophers. It carries centuries in its letterforms. When used for geological copy ("forty million years"), it makes deep time feel physically present.

**Editorial rule**: Cormorant should never be set small. It loses its character below 22px. At small sizes, defer to Inter.

#### Jost — The Cartographer's Hand
**Role**: Brand identity, navigation, section labels, UI labels, domain numbers.
**Weight usage**: Medium (500) for navigation; Regular (400) for labels.
**Case**: Always uppercase with letter-spacing 0.15–0.25em.
**Sizes**: Navigation (13–14px), Labels (10–12px), UI (12–16px).

Jost is a geometric sans with optical clarity — it reads like a cartographer's annotation on a maritime chart. Uppercase, tracked, precise. It is the navigational layer of the visual system: it tells you where you are, not how to feel.

#### Inter — The Scholar's Note
**Role**: Body copy, captions, form elements, long-form educational content.
**Weight usage**: Regular (400) for body; Medium (500) for emphasis.
**Sizes**: Body (15–17px), Caption (12–13px).
**Line height**: 1.65–1.75 (generous — this is reading text, not display text).

Inter is the reading font. It disappears and lets the content dominate. For a brand whose primary value is knowledge transmission, the body font must be maximally legible without character.

### 2.3 — Spatial Grammar

**The void is the message.**

Phaigort's 40–50% negative space is not underdevelopment or restraint. It is the geological equivalent of the space inside a crystal — structurally essential to the material's properties. Remove it and the crystal collapses.

**Spatial principles:**
- Every major composition has one visual centre of gravity, surrounded by void.
- No element competes with another. Elements are choreographed, not crowded.
- The 8px grid (multiples: 8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128) is the geological strata — the underlying order beneath visible surfaces.
- **Caesura** (the dramatic section break between dark and light fields) is a formal device. Each caesura must feel earned, not habitual.
- **The DarkFieldStage** (deep-navy background blocks) is reserved for moments of maximum drama: the hero entry, the collection CTA, the atelier portal. Used too frequently, it loses power.

### 2.5 — Iconographic Vocabulary

Phaigort uses **no emoji icons** and no generic icon libraries. The iconographic vocabulary is architectural and minimal:

- **Dividers**: Thin 1px horizontal rules (`border-royal-navy/10`) — geological strata lines
- **Scroll indicator**: Vertical line dissolving to transparent (gradient from `platinum/30` to transparent) — the thread of descent
- **Domain numbers**: `01`, `02`, `03`, `04` — cartographic annotations
- **Navigation arrows**: Text-based (`→`, `←`) or minimal custom SVG — never icon library arrows
- **Section labels** (Caption): 10–12px Jost uppercase, widely tracked — the map legend
- **The Coral dot**: A single `#FF6B4A` circular mark used as a discovery signal — rare, meaningful

## PART THREE: PHOTOGRAPHY CREATIVE DIRECTIONS

### 3.1 — The Photography Manifesto

> *Photography at Phaigort is not decoration. It is documentation.*

Every image must perform one of three functions:

1. **Reveal geological truth**: Show the internal structure, formation context, or physical properties of the material in a way that words alone cannot.
2. **Establish provenance context**: Ground the material in its historical, cultural, or geographic origin through visual reference (maps, tools, craft environments, geological settings).
3. **Create emotional encounter**: Generate the specific emotional arc (Encounter → Curiosity → Comprehension → Reverence → Desire) through composition, light, and scale.

Photography that only decorates — that places a pretty stone on a pretty background for aesthetic pleasure — does not belong in the Phaigort visual system.

---

### 3.2 — Universal Technical Standards

#### Format Specifications
| Use | Aspect Ratio | Minimum Resolution | File Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hero (full-width) | 16:9 (landscape) | 2400 × 1350px | WebP (lossless) |
| Collection cards | 16:9 | 1600 × 900px | WebP |
| Editorial spreads | 3:2 | 3000 × 2000px | WebP |
| Portrait/detail | 4:5 | 1200 × 1500px | WebP |
| Square detail | 1:1 | 1200 × 1200px | WebP |
| OG/Social | 1.91:1 | 1200 × 630px | JPEG |
| Close-up/macro | 1:1 or 4:3 | 1500 × 1500px | WebP |

#### Color Grading Mandate
- **Shadows**: Warm amber-brown push — never blue-grey shadows
- **Highlights**: Cool platinum-white ceiling — never blown yellow or warm clipping
- **Midtones**: Neutral, allowing material color to dominate
- **Overall temperature**: 4200–4800K (slightly warm, not cold)
- **Saturation**: Material-selective — gemstone colors are preserved at full saturation; backgrounds desaturated toward neutral
- **Film grain**: Light Kodak Vision 3 500T emulation — organic warmth, not digital noise. Grain size: fine (not coarse/photographic).
- **Vignette**: Subtle 5–10% darkening at edges — focuses attention, creates depth. Never aggressive.

#### Depth of Field Policy
- **Macro/detail shots**: Selective focus revealing internal structure. Background: fully out of focus but not distracting (controlled dark/neutral environment).
- **Context shots**: Deep focus (f/8 or equivalent) — environment is as important as object.
- **Editorial portraits**: Medium focus — subject sharp, environment subtly soft.
- **No gratuitous bokeh**: Blurry backgrounds that obscure context are counter to Phaigort's knowledge-transmission mission.

#### Prohibited Elements
- **Never**: Ring boxes, jewellery store props, velvet ring holders
- **Never**: Fashion model hands holding gemstones (Phaigort is not a retail jeweler)
- **Never**: Sparkle/flare filters, prismatic lens flares, artificial rainbow effects
- **Never**: White cyclorama studio backgrounds (cold, anonymous)
- **Never**: Stock photography aesthetic — generic, context-free, anonymous
- **Never**: Price tags, retail packaging, point-of-sale materials
- **Never**: Perfect, clinical light that removes geological character

### 3.3 — Domain I: Geological Rarities (Coloured Gemstones)

#### Visual Mandate
The photograph must show that the stone is a **geological event**. It should appear as if the viewer has been granted access to a laboratory or natural history collection, not a jewellery showcase.

#### Approved Subjects
- Uncut or minimally cut rough crystals: sapphires in matrix, spinel octahedra, alexandrite in pegmatite host rock
- Precisely cut stones on **scientific specimen trays** (not velvet ring holders) — white ceramic dishes, granite slabs, aged brass trays
- Stones photographed against geological maps of their origin region
- Internal characteristics (silk, inclusions, fingerprints) shown via dark-field illumination
- Crystal habit series: showing the crystal form that produced the cut stone

#### Lighting Prescription: Dark-Field Illumination
Dark-field lighting is the geological standard for revealing internal structure in coloured stones. The stone floats on a dark field (deep navy `#0F172A`) illuminated by a single or dual-axis light source positioned to enter the pavilion and refract through the interior. This reveals:
- Silk in Kashmir sapphires (rutile needles that give the velvety character)
- Chromium fluorescence in Burmese rubies and spinels
- The alexandrite colour change (photograph under both daylight 5500K and incandescent 2800K)
- Colour zoning patterns in parti-coloured tourmalines

#### Approved Prop Language
| Prop | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Aged cartographic maps | Origin provenance — Kashmir, Ceylon, Mozambique, Madagascar |
| Brass or bronze scientific instruments (loupes, tweezers, dividers) | Gemological examination context |
| White ceramic specimen dishes | Scientific presentation, not retail |
| Granite or slate surface | Geological substrate |
| Aged linen or vellum | Archive quality, provenance documentation |
| Folded geological survey papers | Origin documentation |
| Glass specimen jars (sealed, museum-style) | Collection and preservation context |

**Prohibited props**: Ring boxes, velvet cushions, champagne glasses, flower petals, water droplets as stylistic choice.

#### Compositional Directions
- **The Geological Float**: Stone centred in dark field, 40% of frame, surrounded by void — maximum 60% negative space. No other elements. Pure material encounter.
- **The Cartographic Context**: Stone laid on an open geological map of its origin, photographed from directly above (plan view). The map text and contour lines partially visible at frame edges.
- **The Examination**: A stone in a gemological loupe or microscope eyepiece — the act of discovery rendered visible. Warm lamplight source, dark surroundings.
- **The Matrix Specimen**: Rough crystal still in host rock, displayed horizontally on a slate surface. Both the crystal and the matrix visible — the geological event intact.
- **The Colour Comparison**: Two versions of the same stone type under different light sources (daylight vs. incandescent), side by side — for alexandrites especially.

#### Copy Directions for Captions
Caption format: `[Species] · [Origin] · [Formation context]`
Example: *"Padparadscha sapphire · Ilakaka, Madagascar · Alluvial deposit, 150 million years"*

### 3.4 — Domain II: Precious Metals

#### Visual Mandate
Precious metal specimens must read as **geological sculpture** — not bullion, not jewellery. The photograph must communicate that gold formed in a hydrothermal vein 65 million years ago is fundamentally different from a gold bar refined yesterday.

#### Approved Subjects
- Native gold specimens: crystalline gold (octahedral, cubic, arborescent forms), wire gold in quartz matrix, nuggets with visible crystal habit
- Platinum group metals in their natural form (iridosmine, sperrylite, platiniferous nuggets)
- Silver dendrites (the branching forms of native silver are among the most sculptural objects in mineralogy)
- Electrum (natural gold-silver alloy with distinctive pale-yellow colour)

#### Lighting Prescription: Raking Sculptural Light
Metal specimens require **raking light** — a single directional light source at a low angle (15–30 degrees above horizontal) that reveals surface topography through extreme highlight and shadow contrast. This lighting:
- Makes crystalline facets of gold sparkle individually
- Shows the arborescent, branching character of dendritic forms
- Creates shadow modelling that communicates three-dimensional form
- Distinguishes polished surfaces from rough matrix

Color temperature: 3500–4000K for gold (warm brings out yellow-red saturation). 4500–5000K for silver and platinum (cooler enhances silver-white character).

#### Compositional Directions
- **The Sculptural Specimen**: Metal specimen on a dark slate or granite base, raking light from camera left or right, photographed slightly above subject level. The specimen reads as sculpture. 
- **The Alluvial Pan**: A gold pan (traditional prospecting equipment) with fine gold dust and small nuggets in water, photographed from above — the gold as geographic accumulation, not manufactured object.
- **The Geological Context**: Crystalline gold still in its quartz matrix, displayed with a cross-section showing the vein structure. Context tells the formation story.
- **The Weight**: Native gold specimen on a brass scientific scale, weights alongside — the geological weight versus manufactured weight. The number as narrative.
- **The Electrum Comparison**: Native electrum (pale gold) displayed beside native gold (deep yellow) and native silver (white) — the spectrum of natural metal composition visible.

#### Surface Treatment Notes
Gold in photography tends to either blow out (lose all detail) or saturate into orange. Correct exposure for the gold mid-tones; allow specular highlights to briefly clip. The deep shadows in crystal cavities are as important as the bright facets.

### 3.5 — Domain III: Historical Artifacts

#### Visual Mandate
Historical artifacts carry **trade routes in their materials**. A Portuguese filigree pendant carries in its wire-work the three-century refinement of the goldsmithing tradition. A Spanish colonial piece carries the metallurgy of Andean mines. The photography must make the history physically visible — not as nostalgia, but as evidence.

#### Approved Subjects
- Portuguese filigree (granulation and wirework — 65-step tradition)
- Spanish colonial goldwork (Andean metalwork, Lima workshops)
- Manila Galleon-era trade pieces (Asian export for the European market)
- Pre-Columbian metal objects (with full documentation)
- Antique gem-set pieces from documented European estate collections
- Asian court jewellery of documented provenance

#### Lighting Prescription: Archival Documentary Light
The archival tradition (museum photography, auction house photography, rare book photography) uses **flat, even illumination** with controlled shadow detail. This is not the dramatic raking light of specimens — it is the documentation light of a conservator.

- Two-source soft illumination at 45 degrees (left and right of subject)
- Fill light to eliminate harsh shadows while preserving surface modelling
- Color temperature: 5000K (D50 — the international standard for colour-critical evaluation)
- White card bounce to maintain platinum-white highlight ceiling

#### Compositional Directions
- **The Archive Photograph**: Object centred on aged linen or aged vellum, photographed from directly above (plan view). A provenance tag or cartouche partially visible at the edge. The object is both beautiful and documented.
- **The Craft Detail**: Extreme macro close-up of wirework, granulation, or setting technique — the human skill made visible at a scale the naked eye does not normally perceive.
- **The Trade Route Spread**: Object displayed on an open period map showing its origin trade route. A spice jar, a navigation instrument, a language primer alongside — the ecosystem of the Iberian Voyage made tangible.
- **The Inheritance Series**: Three generations of an artifact's material journey — rough metal, worked form, finished object — displayed in left-to-right sequence.
- **The Comparative Provenance**: Two objects of similar type but different origin displayed side by side, showing how trade routes influenced design vocabulary.

#### Prop Language for Historical Domain
| Prop | Reference |
|---|---|
| Aged cartographic maps (Iberian, Portuguese, Spanish) | Garcia de Orta / Manila Galleon routes |
| Aged leather-bound books (open to relevant pages) | Natural philosophy tradition |
| Wax seals and sealing wax | Provenance documentation |
| Brass compass or astrolabe | Navigation and discovery |
| Period ledger paper or vellum (folded) | Trade documentation |
| Natural fiber (raw silk, raw cotton) | Trade material context |
| Wooden crates or trays (aged, unvarnished) | Cargo and transport |

### 3.6 — Domain IV: Contemporary Innovations

#### Visual Mandate
Contemporary material innovations occupy the **boundary between science and aesthetics**. A metamorphic composite or aerospace alloy is an innovation that required years of research to achieve properties previously considered impossible. The photography must honour both the scientific precision and the aesthetic surprise.

#### Approved Subjects
- Metamorphic composites with visible layer structure
- Reactive surface treatments (materials that change appearance under light angle)
- Aerospace-grade alloys with non-industrial finishing
- Materials with extreme property combinations (simultaneously hard and ductile, thermally stable and visually dynamic)
- Custom structural finishes developed exclusively for Phaigort application

#### Lighting Prescription: Studio Geometric
For contemporary innovations, the studio environment is appropriate — but it should be a **laboratory studio**, not a commercial product studio.

- **Raking multi-axis light** to reveal surface topography and reactive properties
- **Polarised light** for surfaces with birefringent or iridescent properties
- **UV illumination** for materials with fluorescent response (photograph in UV and visible light)
- Dark field OR platinum-white background — never warm tones (contemporary innovations live in cool precision)
- Color temperature: 5500–6000K (daylight, precise, controlled)

#### Compositional Directions
- **The Property Demonstration**: A single material slab or panel photographed at three different angles — showing how its appearance changes with light direction. A visual argument for the material's reactive properties.
- **The Microscale-to-Macroscale**: Electron microscope image of the material's structure alongside the macroscopic object. Microstructure making visual sense of surface appearance.
- **The Impossible Property**: A material that appears simultaneously rigid and fluid, hard and soft, opaque and translucent — the photograph captures the paradox. A single frame.
- **The Development Documentation**: Prototype and final object alongside material specification data — presented as a laboratory exhibit. The process is the story.
- **The Context Inversion**: The contemporary material placed in an archival setting (on aged linen, beside a period instrument) — the dialogue between geological time and human innovation time.

## PART FOUR: MAGAZINE EDITORIAL DIRECTIONS

### 4.1 — Editorial Philosophy

Phaigort's editorial material should reference the tradition of **luxury natural history publications** — the illustrated volumes of the 18th and 19th centuries where scientific accuracy and aesthetic beauty were inseparable. *Les Pierres Précieuses* (Bauer, 1896), *Gems and Gem Minerals* (Dana, 1892), the Sibley Guide tradition — these are the editorial ancestors.

The closest living reference: **Apartamento** (for spatial warmth), **032c** (for intellectual rigor), **T: The New York Times Style Magazine** (for luxury editorial precision), and **Mineral Messengers** (for geological specificity).

**Not**: Vogue Joaillerie, Robb Report, Town & Country — those represent the luxury establishment that Phaigort is providing an alternative to.

---

### 4.2 — Cover Concepts

#### Cover A: "The Stone"
A single unheated Kashmir sapphire, photographed on a pure platinum-white field, occupying 40% of the frame. Below: `PHAIGORT` in Jost uppercase, 0.25em tracking. A single datum line: *"Kashmir · Corundum · 65,000,000 years"*. Nothing else. Maximum void. The stone earns its prominence through absence of competition.

#### Cover B: "The Map"
An aged cartographic map of the Iberian trade routes (Manila Galleon line highlighted in Coral `#FF6B4A` — a single stroke). In the lower quadrant: a gemstone specimen laid directly on the map at the location of its geological origin. `PHAIGORT` reversed in platinum over the deep-navy ocean area of the map. An editorial caption in Jost: *"From source to collection — the Iberian Voyage, continued."*

## PART SIX: MOTION & INTERACTION DIRECTION

### 6.1 — Cinematic Principles

Animation at Phaigort follows the principle of **geological pace** — the tempo of processes that take millions of years made perceptible at the scale of seconds.

- **Reveal speed**: Always slower than instinct says. 1.2s for major reveals (not 0.3s). Patience signals depth.
- **Easing**: `[0.22, 1, 0.36, 1]` (custom cubic-bezier) — a slow, powerful start, rapid arrival, immediate settle. The energy of a stone dropping into water.
- **Delay stagger**: 0.1–0.15s between siblings. Like geological layers depositing one at a time.
- **No bounce**: Nothing in the Phaigort universe bounces. Geological time does not bounce.
- **No spring**: No spring physics. Controlled, directed, precise.

### 6.2 — Entry Animation Library

```
// The DarkField Reveal — for hero content on navy background
initial: { opacity: 0, y: 24 }
animate: { opacity: 1, y: 0 }
transition: { duration: 1.2, ease: [0.22, 1, 0.36, 1] }

// The Domain Card Rise — for collection cards
initial: { opacity: 0, y: 24 }
animate: isInView ? { opacity: 1, y: 0 } : {}
transition: { delay: 0.1 + i * 0.12, duration: 0.8, ease: [0.22, 1, 0.36, 1] }

// The Pillar Entry — for three-column content blocks
initial: { opacity: 0, y: 24 }
animate: isInView ? { opacity: 1, y: 0 } : {}
transition: { delay: 0.2 + i * 0.15, duration: 0.8, ease: [0.22, 1, 0.36, 1] }

// The Image Reveal — for photography unveiling
initial: clipPath 'inset(100% 0 0 0)'
animate: clipPath 'inset(0% 0 0 0)'
transition: { delay, duration: 1.0, ease: [0.22, 1, 0.36, 1] }
```

### 6.3 — Scroll-Driven Effects

- **Hero parallax**: Content shifts `[0, 40px]` as hero scrolls out. Creates sense of the text floating above the material.
- **Overlay parallax**: Background overlay shifts `[0%, 15%]` — slower than content, creating depth separation.
- **ScrollReveal threshold**: `-100px` margin (triggers just before element enters viewport — anticipatory reveal, not reactive).
- **ScrollProgress indicator**: The thin coral `#FF6B4A` line at page top grows with scroll depth — a geological survey marker.

### 6.4 — Micro-interaction Grammar

- **Link hover**: `color` transition 300ms — no scale, no transform. The text darkens or lightens. Material-appropriate restraint.
- **Button hover**: Background shifts 200ms. The button does not move. It changes state.
- **Card hover**: `scale(1.05)` on the image inside the card only (not the card itself). The frame stays. The material breathes.
- **The Marquee**: ManifestoMarquee at constant, slow velocity (`duration: 40s`). Like tectonic plates — always in motion, never dramatic.
- **The Glowing Radial (PrivateAccessCTA)**: Ambient pulsation `scale: [1, 1.05, 1], opacity: [0.3, 0.5, 0.3]` over 8 seconds. The deep heartbeat of geological pressure.
